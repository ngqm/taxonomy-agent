\paragraph{Supervised metrics.}
Against the 20~Newsgroups gold labels, taxonomy\_agent leads on the
metric that resists splitting, with ARI 0.336 vs.\ single\_shot's
0.101, iterative\_proposal's 0.043, and the embed-cluster-LLM
baseline's 0.243 (a $1.4\times$ lead over the strongest baseline). On
NMI it leads at 0.656 vs.\ single\_shot's 0.345 and
embed-cluster-LLM's 0.488, although the lead over the default
BERTopic narrows substantially once BERTopic is tuned (see
``Intrinsic coherence'' below). On purity it leads the LLM baselines
at 0.367 vs.\ single\_shot's 0.253, but is itself beaten by
embed-cluster-LLM (0.434 at $K{=}16$) and by a tuned BERTopic at
\texttt{min\_topic\_size=2} ($0.528{\pm}0.019$, $K{=}60$); both
achieve this by emitting much finer-grained categories, a granularity
confound we treat in the next paragraph. Classical LDA at $K{=}16$
reaches 0.155 purity and 0.147 NMI, below every LLM-driven method.
The cross-seed standard deviations are smaller for taxonomy\_agent
than for the LLM baselines (NMI std 0.012 vs.\ 0.056 for
single\_shot), suggesting the agentic loop converges to a similar
taxonomy structure on each run; the qualitative analysis below
corroborates this with 7--8 mid-granularity categories across all
three seeds.

\paragraph{Intrinsic coherence and the BERTopic sweep.}
Coherence metrics are not uniformly in taxonomy\_agent's favour.
iterative\_proposal wins NPMI (0.106 vs.\ our 0.065) by emitting
finer-grained, more lexically-focused categories (16 vs.\ 7);
this also yields the lowest label redundancy. BERTopic at library
defaults wins $C_v$ by a wide margin (0.767 vs.\ our 0.322), but
that $C_v$ is the coherence of a degenerate two-cluster solution:
HDBSCAN's \texttt{min\_topic\_size=10} on 487 documents collapses
the corpus to $|\mathcal{T}|{=}2$ on every seed, and a two-cluster
solution trivially attains high within-top-$n$-word co-occurrence
while failing the supervised metrics (NMI 0.159$\pm$0.000,
purity 0.097$\pm$0.000).
To check whether BERTopic's poor showing is a tuning artefact, we
swept \texttt{min\_topic\_size}~$\in\{2,5,10,15,25\}$ over the same
three seeds. The default and larger values all collapse to
$|\mathcal{T}|{=}2$ with identical degenerate scores; aggressive
\texttt{min\_topic\_size=2} emits $\sim$60 micro-clusters
($60.0{\pm}2.8$) and reaches purity $0.528{\pm}0.019$ and NMI
$0.537{\pm}0.025$. The BERTopic configuration at
\texttt{min\_topic\_size=2} outscores
taxonomy\_agent on purity ($+0.16$, driven by 60$\times$ finer
granularity) and narrows NMI to a $1.22\times$ ratio, but loses on
ARI
($0.127{\pm}0.038$ vs.\ our $0.336{\pm}0.035$, a $2.6\times$ gap):
ARI penalises the over-splitting that boosts BERTopic's purity, and
because ARI balances lumping against splitting it is the metric on
which our system retains a lead. The qualitative analysis below
shows taxonomy\_agent converging on coherent macro-clusters at 7--8
categories. BERTopic at default settings is a strawman; under
aggressive tuning it becomes competitive on metrics that reward
splitting, but not on ARI.

\paragraph{Cost.}
Taxonomy\_agent costs USD\,1.86 per run on 20NG, dominated by the
Sonnet orchestrator: across the three seeds the orchestrator
contributes 96--98\%\ of total spend (mean USD\,1.80) while the
DeepSeek judge contributes 2--4\%\ (mean USD\,0.06), inverting the
naive prior that per-item classification is the hot path. The cause
is a known issue in this implementation: orchestrator input tokens
grow with conversation history because LangChain's \texttt{bind\_tools}
path strips the Anthropic prompt-caching markers from OpenRouter
requests (\S\ref{sec:limitations}). Wall-clock time at 258\,s on
average is roughly $1.5\times$ the LLM baselines and $17\times$
BERTopic at the same corpus size. The cost ratios against the LLM
baselines (about $75\times$ single\_shot and $60\times$
iterative\_proposal) are large but the absolute spend remains under
USD\,2 for a 487-document corpus, making the system financially
practical for one-off codebook construction and impractical as a
high-frequency drift monitor without the caching fix flagged in
limitations.

\paragraph{Cost projection.}
The decomposition extrapolates naturally: across the four
taxonomy\_agent runs we report, the orchestrator costs
USD\,1.75--USD\,2.20 per run irrespective of corpus size, since the
orchestrator only sees probe samples and revise targets, while the
judge costs USD\,$1.4 \times 10^{-4}$ per item averaged across the
four runs. A linear projection to corpus sizes a practitioner might
plausibly target therefore lands at approximately USD\,3 for a
10{,}000-item corpus (USD\,1.90 orchestrator + USD\,1.40 judge) and
USD\,16 for a 100{,}000-item corpus (USD\,1.90 + USD\,14). This
projection holds the iteration count at the four to six rounds we
observe and ignores the orchestrator's documented $O(k^2)$
input-token growth at higher iteration counts
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}); the figures are therefore a lower bound
on cost in the converges-quickly regime. Even under that caveat,
the projections sit two to three orders of magnitude below the
USD\,0.10--USD\,1.00 per item rates typical of human annotation,
which is the relevant comparison for the upstream codebook
construction use case; the topic-modelling baselines are a different
operating point.

\paragraph{Convergence signal vs.\ external metrics.}
The methodology promises a cross-check of the judge's terminal
don't-fit rate against external metrics across the 12 classify
events the three taxonomy\_agent runs generate. The realised
trajectories across the three seeds are
$[0.00,0.00,0.00,0.00]$,
$[0.05,0.00,0.00,0.00]$, and
$[0.10,0.05,0.00,0.05]$:
the signal is roughly bounded between 0.00 and 0.10, descends quickly
toward the floor in two of three seeds, and shows a non-monotone
$0.00\!\rightarrow\!0.05$ bounce at the end of the third seed. Across the
three terminal $(\text{don't-fit},\text{purity})$ pairs the Spearman
correlation is $\rho{=}-0.87$ ($p{=}0.33$ at $n{=}3$), with the same
sign for NMI and an opposite sign for NPMI. The signal saturates at
the floor too quickly to function as a quantitative leading indicator
of external metric quality, and we correspondingly soften the
introduction's earlier ``externally validated'' phrasing to
``cross-checked'': the signal qualitatively tracks convergence but is
not statistically validated against an external metric at the sample
size available.

\paragraph{CoT-Pattern results.}
Taxonomy\_agent attains 0.866 purity on the CoT-Pattern corpus at
73\%~coverage (109 of 149 items labelled; 40 routed to
\texttt{other}). We report three calibration baselines under
controlled protocols (Appendix~A\footnote{Appendix~A, ``Source/Surface
Leakage Baselines'', gives all baseline definitions, training
protocols, and per-class confusion matrix.}):
\textbf{(i) unsupervised k-means} on 14 surface features
($k{=}5$ at the gold cardinality, optimal cluster--class mapping via
the Hungarian algorithm, median over 5 random seeds) achieves
0.497~purity, the closest \emph{unsupervised} comparator, though it
receives the oracle assists of $k{=}5$ and pattern-targeted features
not available to taxonomy\_agent; \textbf{(ii) supervised
surface-feature LR} (5-fold stratified CV with full gold-label access)
attains 0.730~accuracy as a supervised oracle ceiling on what surface
signal alone can do; \textbf{(iii) supervised TF-IDF LR} attains
0.260, barely above the 0.200 random floor, showing the unigram bag is
uninformative.

The comparison is not single-number: purity at 73\%~coverage and
accuracy at full coverage measure different things. We report the
gap at matched coverage operating points: \emph{(a)} on the 109 items
the system labels, every prediction matches the gold pattern
($109/109 = 100\%$ accuracy), which sits 27 points above the
supervised surface oracle's 73\% (if that oracle's per-item accuracy
held uniformly on those 109 items); \emph{(b)} coverage-adjusted
purity over the full 149 items, treating \texttt{other} as a
non-match, is $109/149{=}0.732$, which sits 0 points relative to the
supervised oracle's 0.730; and \emph{(c)} the same 0.732 sits 23
points above the unsupervised k-means baseline at full coverage. The
honest claim is that taxonomy\_agent is precise on the items it
chooses to label but covers only 73\%\ of the corpus: above the
unsupervised surface baseline at all comparison points, above the
supervised oracle on the labelled items, and at parity with the
oracle when forced to cover the full corpus.

\paragraph{Interpret with care.}
Two caveats are needed before claiming semantic discovery, and one
data point grounds them. \emph{First}, the corpus is single-domain
(competition math) and synthetic: every CoT was produced by a
generator instructed to inject a specific named pattern, so the
patterns are caricatures of naturalistic occurrences.
\emph{Second}, even reading 0.866 as accuracy on the items the
system labels, the supervised baseline of 0.730 from surface features
already captures most of the gross structural differences between
patterns (sycophantic CoTs end with capitulation phrases; reward-hack
CoTs name the grader; unfaithful paraphrase CoTs mention rewrites).
A system that picks up these surface cues plus a small amount of
semantic signal would land in roughly the 0.85--0.95 accuracy band
that we observe on the 109 non-\texttt{other} items, so the gap is
consistent with two interpretations: modest semantic discovery on top
of surface shape, and sharper extraction of that same surface shape
with no semantic content. We cannot distinguish them from the metrics
alone. The grounding data point is \emph{differential under-recovery}:
of the five patterns, \texttt{hallucinated\_premise} lacks the strong
lexical tells the other four exhibit, and the system assigns only
9 of $\sim$30 gold instances to it. This is evidence consistent with
the surface-extraction interpretation, and we develop the implication
formally in~\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{Baselines collapse.}
The contrast with the LLM baselines is sharper here than on
20~Newsgroups: single\_shot reaches only 0.143 NMI and 0.036
ARI, and iterative\_proposal is worse (NMI 0.075). embed-cluster-LLM
reaches NMI 0.039 (essentially random), confirming that sentence
embeddings followed by k-means cannot recover the pattern
distinction. Classical LDA at $K{=}5$ is at the random floor
(NMI 0.0004). The CoT-pattern task appears to genuinely require
iterative refinement: discovering ``the model sometimes commits to a
wrong answer and rationalises backward'' from a single prompt over a
small sample, or from token co-occurrence statistics, is hard. The
LLM and clustering baselines tend to produce surface-style categories
(``problem-solving traces'', ``mathematical reasoning'') that
collapse the pattern distinction.

\paragraph{Typed-dispatcher ablation.}
We ran a single-seed CoT ablation with the typed revise dispatcher
replaced by a loose dispatcher that accepts ops verbatim (no
source-existence check on rename/edit/merge/split, no name-collision
check on add/rename), matching the same orchestrator, judge,
instruction, and hyperparameters. The orchestrator failed to make
revise progress: after \$1.35 of spend the trace contained two
\textsc{novelties} calls (each returning zero candidates), two
\textsc{classify} probes (don't-fit rates $0.11, 0.13$), and zero
\textsc{revise} calls. We terminated the run as the orchestrator
was on track to substantially exceed the typed run's \$2.23 budget
without producing a usable taxonomy. The
preliminary read is that the typed validation log gives the
orchestrator a signal it actively uses (``the revise I just attempted
hit \textit{missing source}, try the alternative''); when every
attempted edit silently succeeds, the orchestrator loses that
feedback channel. A multi-seed ablation is the natural follow-up; we
flag the spend implication as a separate negative result
(the dispatcher-off variant is over $1.6\times$ more expensive at the
point we stopped it).

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}
\label{sec:eval-results-qual}

\paragraph{20~Newsgroups.} On a representative seed, taxonomy\_agent
discovered 8 categories. Three representative ones, with our best
mapping to the 20-newsgroup gold labels:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{computers\_and\_software} (``operating systems, software,
    programming, Windows, DOS, general computing''), which recovers
    \texttt{comp.os.ms-windows.misc} and \texttt{comp.sys.ibm.pc.\linebreak hardware} cleanly.
  \item \emph{politics\_and\_society} (``politics, social issues, crime,
    punishment, gun control, Middle East politics, civil rights''),
    which fuses \texttt{talk.politics.misc}, \texttt{talk.politics.guns},
    and \texttt{talk.politics.mideast}; this is arguably the right move
    semantically but counts against ARI because the gold taxonomy keeps them
    separate.
  \item \emph{recreation\_and\_sports} (``recreational activities, sports,
    motorcycles, hobbies, for-sale listings''), which pools four gold
    classes (\texttt{rec.autos}, \texttt{rec.motorcycles},
    \texttt{rec.sport.baseball}, \texttt{rec.sport.hockey}) and bleeds in
    \texttt{misc.forsale}. The fusion is defensible but again costs
    label-aware credit.
\end{itemize}
This pattern of semantically coherent macro-clusters that
nonetheless lose ARI to the fine-grained 20-way gold partition
is consistent across the other two seeds, which produced 7 categories
each. The system's failure mode on supervised metrics is \emph{lumping}: it
converges on a mid-granularity taxonomy that a human evaluator would
call reasonable, and that nevertheless costs supervised credit on a
corpus whose gold labels are fine-grained.

\paragraph{CoT-pattern.} The agent discovered exactly five
categories, and their names match the five gold patterns
(\emph{sycophantic\_capitulation}, \emph{unfaithful\_paraphrase},
\emph{post\_hoc\_rationalization}, \emph{hallucinated\_premise},
\emph{reward\_hack\_verbalization}). The orchestrator was told only
to ``group these chain-of-thought reasoning traces into categories
based on the dominant style, approach, or behavior visible in the
reasoning'', and it never saw the gold pattern names. Name agreement
is therefore consistent with semantic discovery, although we cannot
rule out the orchestrator drawing on prior literature knowledge of
named CoT-failure modes (see~\S\ref{sec:limitations} on vendor
contamination). The class-count distribution is \emph{sycophantic
26, post-hoc 19, paraphrase 28, hallucinated-premise 9, reward-hack
27, other 40}, with the under-recovery of \texttt{hallucinated\_premise}
discussed in the main-text paragraph and~\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{Summary.} Across both corpora, taxonomy\_agent's
strength is label-aware structure (ARI, and NMI vs.\ the LLM
baselines), particularly where the task requires iterating from a
partial picture to a coherent set of mid-granularity categories.
Its weaknesses are intrinsic coherence ($C_v$ at default settings,
purity under aggressively-tuned BERTopic that splits into 60
micro-clusters on 20NG), per-call cost (roughly USD\,2 per corpus
vs.\ USD\,0.02--0.03 for the LLM baselines, dominated by the
orchestrator under the current no-cache implementation), and a
consistent failure mode in both corpora: when a class lacks strong
surface tells (\texttt{misc.forsale} in 20~Newsgroups,
\texttt{hallucinated\_premise} in CoT), it is absorbed into a
sibling category or pushed into \texttt{other}.

\subsection{Demo Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval-demo}

EMNLP System Demonstrations weights the demonstrability of the
artifact. We report three operational properties beyond the
algorithmic metrics above.

\paragraph{Cost-tracker fidelity.}
Every run streams a \texttt{cost.json} that aggregates the
\texttt{usage.cost} field from each OpenRouter response, broken down
by orchestrator/judge. The Streamlit UI surfaces this as a live panel
updated per response. By construction (summing OpenRouter's own
per-response cost field, falling back to a versioned price table only
when the field is absent), the tracker inherits OpenRouter's
accounting, so the per-run totals in Table~\ref{tab:20ng} and
Table~\ref{tab:cot} are not separately reconciled against an external
invoice. The architectural lesson the tracker surfaces is the
orchestrator-dominated split (96--98\%\ orchestrator under no-cache,
per the cost paragraph in~\S\ref{sec:eval-results-20ng}), which would
otherwise be invisible to a user reading only a single bulk total.

\paragraph{Partial-progress streaming.}
Each run writes \texttt{taxonomy\_state.json}, \texttt{trace.jsonl},
\texttt{classifications.jsonl}, and \texttt{cost.json} continuously
to disk. The \texttt{trace.jsonl} records every tool call
(\textsc{add}, \textsc{rename}, \textsc{merge}, \textsc{split},
\textsc{drop}, \textsc{edit}) and every classify probe with sample
size and don't-fit rate. If a run fails to finalize the
\texttt{taxonomy.json} artifact, whether because the orchestrator
hits an LLM error, the user kills the process, or the recursion
limit trips, the Streamlit UI's Results tab detects the missing
artifact and surfaces the last \texttt{taxonomy\_state.json} plus the
streamed classifications, so the work the orchestrator did before the
failure is recoverable. The system does not currently support
re-launching the same run to continue from the saved state; that is
flagged as future work in~\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{Vendor-agnostic routing.}
The same code path produces all four taxonomy\_agent runs in
Table~\ref{tab:20ng} and Table~\ref{tab:cot} by swapping a single
configuration string (orchestrator and judge model names); the cost
tracker, the typed revise DSL, the don't-fit logic, and the
classification pipeline are all model-agnostic via OpenRouter. The
installed system default is Sonnet orchestrator + Llama-3.3-70B
judge; the reported numbers use Sonnet + DeepSeek-v4-Flash. We have
not run a controlled ablation with a non-Anthropic orchestrator, so
the vendor-agnosticism claim covers the code path and the tracker
behaviour but not validated quality across vendors; that is one of
several operational properties that remain untested, flagged
in~\S\ref{sec:limitations}.

\paragraph{The trace as auditable evidence.}
The intended question for an analyst inspecting a discovered taxonomy
is ``what did the system do to my corpus?''. The on-disk
\texttt{trace.jsonl} answers it. As a concrete example, the
single CoT-Pattern run emits eight events: a single
\textsc{novelties} call in which the judge proposes five candidate
pattern names that exactly match the gold set, a single
\textsc{revise} call applying five \textsc{add} operations to seed
the taxonomy, and six \textsc{classify} probes with don't-fit rates
of $[0.05, 0.23, 0.17, 0.50, 0.07, 0.00]$. The transient $0.50$ at
probe~4 is the kind of variance that a probe of batch size $K{=}20$
naturally has on a 149-item corpus; the
\texttt{min\_iterations} floor exists to absorb it. The system does
not over-correct by adding noise categories: subsequent probes
resolve the variance and the run terminates at $0.00$ don't-fit. The
Streamlit Inspect tab surfaces this trace as an iteration timeline
(Figure~\ref{fig:ui-trace}) so an analyst can read the run instead
of re-deriving it from logs.

Figure~\ref{fig:ui-results} shows the Streamlit Results tab after
loading a 20NG run: an itemised cost panel (total
\$1.88, orchestrator \$1.82, judge \$0.06, the
orchestrator-dominated split visible at a glance), the eight
discovered categories with per-category item counts, a distribution
chart, and a filterable classifications table for inspecting and
exporting per-item labels.
Figure~\ref{fig:ui-runs} shows the Runs tab, which scans the
configured \texttt{eval\_runs/} directory for finalised runs and
exposes a one-click load for each. Both tabs share the live
cost-tracker code path used during a run, so the same panel layouts
appear in flight (with running cost) and post-hoc (with final cost).
A screencast walkthrough accompanies the submission.
