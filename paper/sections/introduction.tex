\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Constructing a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled corpus is a recurring
bottleneck in applied NLP. Annotation guidelines for supervised
classification, codebooks for qualitative research, summarisation schemas
for analyst-facing dashboards, and policy hierarchies for content
moderation all begin life as a manual taxonomy. Producing one typically
requires domain experts to read hundreds of documents, draft candidate
categories, argue over overlaps, and iterate until coverage and
disjointness are acceptable. The process is slow, expensive, and difficult
to reproduce, and the resulting taxonomy is often a brittle artefact that
nobody is incentivised to revisit when the underlying corpus drifts.

Recent work has shown that large language models (LLMs) can replace
much of this manual labour. TopicGPT~\citep{pham2024topicgpt} prompts an
LLM to generate topic names and definitions directly from sampled
documents (including a refinement pass), while
TnT-LLM~\citep{wan2024tntllm} stages a two-phase pipeline that first
summarises a corpus into candidate labels and then distils a
lightweight classifier. Iterative LLM edit loops also appear in
goal-driven and human-in-the-loop systems such as
GoalEx~\citep{wang2023goalex}, LLooM~\citep{lam2024lloom}, and
Co-DETECT~\citep{codetect2025}. Against this backdrop, our contribution
is best read as a careful engineering recomposition rather than a
wholly new mechanism: we narrow the orchestrator's edit surface to a
typed validated DSL with per-op failure isolation, decouple
labelling into a parallel judge, and ship the result with operational
tooling (live cost accounting, partial-progress saving, a Streamlit UI).
These are deliberate practical choices for an operational demo, not
claims of architectural novelty.

We present \textsc{taxonomy\_agent}, an open-source system that
packages these choices. The system is structured as a two-tier agent: a
capable \emph{orchestrator} LLM holds long-horizon state and proposes
edits via a six-tool interface, while a cheap \emph{judge} LLM runs in
parallel to score items, flag don't-fit cases, and propose novelties.
Edits are constrained to a typed revise DSL with six operations
(add, rename, edit, merge, split, drop), each validated before
mutation, with per-op failure isolation. Convergence is self-reported
via the judge's don't-fit rate with a minimum iteration floor and an
explicit escape hatch; we cross-check this self-report against external
purity and NMI on 20~Newsgroups, but stop short of claiming
the don't-fit rate is a quantitatively validated leading indicator.
Empirically, the system dominates LLM and BERTopic baselines on
20~Newsgroups (NMI 0.656, $1.9\times$ next-best), while on the
synthetic CoT-Pattern corpus it improves over a 14-feature surface
baseline (0.866 vs.\ 0.73 purity on the same 149 items) by a margin
that, as we discuss in \S\ref{sec:eval-results-cot}, the metrics alone
cannot cleanly attribute to semantic discovery rather than sharper
surface extraction.
The system is built on LangGraph and OpenRouter, prefers OpenRouter's
native \texttt{usage.cost} field over static price tables, streams
partial progress to disk so that long runs are interruptible, and ships
with a Streamlit UI for live inspection of cost, taxonomy state, and
per-item classifications. Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A two-tier orchestrator--judge architecture that separates
        expensive sequential reasoning from cheap parallel scoring.
  \item A typed six-op revise DSL with validate-before-mutate
        semantics, exposed to the orchestrator as tool calls.
  \item A self-reported convergence signal (don't-fit rate, minimum
        iteration floor, escape hatch) externally validated against
        standard topic-coherence and clustering metrics.
  \item An operational demo: native cost accounting, partial-progress
        saving, and a Streamlit UI, distributed as an open-source
        Python package that is model-agnostic via OpenRouter.
\end{itemize}
