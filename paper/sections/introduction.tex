\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Constructing a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled corpus is a recurring
bottleneck in applied NLP. Annotation guidelines for supervised
classification, codebooks for qualitative research, summarisation schemas
for analyst-facing dashboards, and policy hierarchies for content
moderation all begin life as a manual taxonomy. Producing one typically
requires domain experts to read hundreds of documents, draft candidate
categories, argue over overlaps, and iterate until coverage and
disjointness are acceptable. The process is slow, expensive, and difficult
to reproduce, and the resulting taxonomy is often a brittle artefact that
nobody is incentivised to revisit when the underlying corpus drifts.

Recent work has shown that large language models (LLMs) can replace much
of this manual labour. TopicGPT~\citep{pham2024topicgpt} prompts an LLM
to generate topic names and definitions from sampled documents with a
refinement pass, while TnT-LLM~\citep{wan2024tntllm} stages a two-phase
pipeline that summarises a corpus into candidate labels and then distils
a lightweight classifier. Co-DETECT~\citep{codetect2025} is last year's
EMNLP System Demonstration on collaborative codebook construction with
LLMs and the closest peer to our submission. However, these pipelines
either fold the proposer and the labeller into a single agent that can
hallucinate and force its own labels, or they require a seed codebook or
a human in the loop.

To address this gap, we present \textsc{TaxonomyAgent}, an open-source
orchestrator--judge pipeline that discovers a labelled taxonomy from
scratch over an unlabelled corpus and assigns every item to one
discovered category. An orchestrator LLM proposes typed edits to a
working taxonomy through a dispatcher that validates every precondition
before mutating state and that records per-operation failures without
rolling back successful sibling edits. A stateless judge LLM labels each
item against the current taxonomy from item text alone, with no access
to the orchestrator's tool-call history or prior label assignments. The
judge runs on the cheap model and is called once per item, so per-item
inference cost is linear in corpus size and the orchestrator cannot
force a label past the judge.

Empirically, at the install default (DeepSeek-v4-Flash for both
orchestrator and judge), \textsc{TaxonomyAgent} reaches NMI $0.740
\pm 0.055$ and ARI $0.489 \pm 0.085$ on 20~Newsgroups, beating a
one-shot LLM baseline by $2.1\times$ on NMI and $4.8\times$ on ARI
and the published TopicGPT pipeline by $1.22\times$ on NMI and
$1.65\times$ on ARI (\S\ref{sec:eval-results-20ng}). A Sonnet 4.6
orchestrator pair at eleven times the cost reaches NMI $0.656 \pm
0.012$ and does not improve over the cheap pair, so we make the
cheap pair the publication and install default. On a synthetic
CoT-Pattern corpus the system reaches NMI $0.944 \pm 0.081$ across
three seeds, labelling on average $142$ of $149$ items at $100\%$
accuracy (\S\ref{sec:eval-results-cot}).

Our contributions are fourfold. First, we introduce a stateless
re-deriving judge: the labelling LLM sees only the current taxonomy
and the item text, never the orchestrator's reasoning or prior
labels, so the orchestrator cannot force a label past it and
inference cost is linear in the corpus on the cheap model. Second,
we introduce a typed dispatcher with validate-before-mutate and
per-operation failure isolation: each taxonomy edit is checked
against every precondition before any state changes, per-op failures
within a batch do not roll back successful sibling ops, and a
malformed edit returns the unchanged taxonomy with a log entry the
orchestrator can read on the next step. Third, we empirically
demonstrate that the cheap model in both roles dominates: on 20NG
and CoT-Pattern, DeepSeek-v4-Flash for both orchestrator and judge
beats every comparator including a Claude Sonnet 4.6 orchestrator
pair at eleven times the cost on 20NG, so orchestrator quality is
not the bottleneck at this scale. Fourth, we release an open-source
artefact at \repourl{} with a \texttt{pip install} path, the
install-default configuration used in the paper, and a Streamlit
Inspect tab that replays the trace, partial taxonomies, and cost
split of any run.
