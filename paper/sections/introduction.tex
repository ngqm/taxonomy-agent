\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Discovering structure in a collection of unstructured texts is a
recurring bottleneck across many analyst workflows. An alignment
researcher reads hundreds of chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning traces
to find shared failure modes; a content moderator drafts a category
schema for policy violations; a domain expert constructs annotation
guidelines for supervised classification; a market analyst builds a
codebook of customer complaints. All begin with the same loop: read
samples, draft candidate categories, iterate until coverage and
disjointness are acceptable. The process is slow, expensive, and
difficult to reproduce, and the resulting taxonomy is often a brittle
artefact that nobody is incentivised to revisit when the underlying
corpus drifts.

Recent work has shown that large language models (LLMs) can replace
much of this labour, but the available systems target one of two
narrow operating points. Topic-modeling pipelines such as
TopicGPT~\citep{pham2024topicgpt} and TnT-LLM~\citep{wan2024tntllm}
discover topics from unlabelled text but offer no mechanism for an
analyst to direct discovery toward a particular axis of variation, so
on a corpus whose useful structure is not its surface topic they
recover the topic instead. Tools for
LLM-output evaluation such as
FaithCoT-Bench~\citep{shen2026faithcot}, the hint taxonomy
in~\citet{chen2025reasoningmodels}, and the CoT monitorability
framework of~\citet{korbak2025cotmonitor} evaluate models against
\emph{known} failure categories; they assume the categories are
given. Multi-agent codebook tools such as
Co-DETECT~\citep{codetect2025} and
Thematic-LM~\citep{qiao2025thematiclm} target collaborative codebook
construction with a human in the loop and a seed sketch. None target
automated discovery of taxonomies along a chosen axis of variation
in unstructured LLM outputs.

To address this gap, we present \textsc{TaxonomyAgent}, an
open-source orchestrator--judge pipeline that discovers a labelled
taxonomy along a user-specified axis. The user supplies the corpus
and a one-sentence goal instruction (for example, ``Group these
prompts by the type of manipulation they attempt'', or ``Identify the
topic of each text''). An orchestrator LLM proposes typed edits to a
working taxonomy through a dispatcher that validates every
precondition before mutating state and that records per-operation
failures without rolling back successful sibling edits. A stateless
judge LLM labels each item against the current taxonomy from item
text alone, with no access to the orchestrator's tool-call history
or prior label assignments. The judge runs on the cheap model and is
called once per item, so per-item inference cost is linear in corpus
size and the orchestrator cannot force a label past the judge.

Empirically, with DeepSeek-v4-Flash for both the orchestrator and the
judge, on DarkBench~\citep{kran2025darkbench}, a
benchmark of 660 prompts that each elicit one of six manipulative
dark patterns from an AI assistant, \textsc{TaxonomyAgent} recovers
the six-category expert taxonomy at NMI $0.927 \pm 0.018$ across three
seeds, with no hint about the number of categories, and names all six
patterns on two of three seeds
(\S\ref{sec:eval-results-darkbench}). Against baselines each asked to group by the same
manipulation type through their own interface (a prompt for TopicGPT,
an instruction-tuned embedding for BERTopic), it leads the strongest
by $1.24\times$ on NMI. On
20~Newsgroups as a classical topic-modeling benchmark,
\textsc{TaxonomyAgent} reaches NMI $0.740 \pm 0.055$ and ARI $0.489
\pm 0.085$, beating a one-shot LLM baseline by $2.1\times$ on NMI
and the published TopicGPT pipeline by $1.22\times$
(\S\ref{sec:eval-results-20ng}). The result holds under a second
cheap model (gemini-3.1-flash-lite, NMI $0.884$ on DarkBench).

Our contributions are fourfold. First, we describe a tool for
discovering taxonomies of unstructured patterns in LLM outputs,
designed for analyst workflows where the categories are not known a
priori and where the analyst wants to direct discovery toward a
particular axis (failure modes, reasoning strategies, error types,
jailbreak patterns). Second, we describe a goal-instruction
mechanism through which the user steers discovery toward that axis,
differentiating our system from topic-modeling pipelines that
surface only the dominant variation. Third, we empirically
demonstrate the system on (a) DarkBench, a real safety benchmark on
which \textsc{TaxonomyAgent} recovers the six-category expert taxonomy
and leads every baseline asked to group by the same manipulation type
through its own interface, and (b) a classical 20~Newsgroups benchmark,
where it matches or exceeds published baselines at a fraction of the
cost. Fourth, we
release an open-source artefact at \repourl{} with a \texttt{pip
install} path, the default configuration used in the paper,
and a Streamlit Inspect tab that replays the trace, partial
taxonomies, and cost split of any run.
