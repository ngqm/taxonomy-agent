\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Discovering structure in a collection of unstructured texts is a
recurring bottleneck across many analyst workflows. An alignment
researcher reads hundreds of chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning traces
to find shared failure modes; a content moderator drafts a category
schema for policy violations; a domain expert constructs annotation
guidelines for supervised classification; a market analyst builds a
codebook of customer complaints. All begin with the same loop: read
samples, draft candidate categories, iterate until coverage and
disjointness are acceptable. The process is slow, expensive, and
difficult to reproduce, and the resulting taxonomy is often a brittle
artefact that nobody is incentivised to revisit when the underlying
corpus drifts.

Recent work shows large language models (LLMs) can replace much of this
labour, but available systems target one of two narrow operating points.
Topic-modeling pipelines~\citep{pham2024topicgpt, wan2024tntllm} discover
topics from unlabelled text but give the analyst no way to direct
discovery toward a particular axis of variation, so on a corpus whose
useful structure is not its surface topic they recover the topic
instead. LLM-output evaluation and multi-agent codebook
tools~\citep{shen2026faithcot, chen2025reasoningmodels,
korbak2025cotmonitor, codetect2025, qiao2025thematiclm} instead assume
the category set is given, or build one from a human seed sketch. None
target automated discovery of a taxonomy along a chosen axis of
variation in unstructured LLM outputs.

To address this gap, we present \textsc{TaxonomyAgent}, an
open-source orchestrator--judge pipeline that discovers a labelled
taxonomy along a user-specified axis. The user supplies the corpus
and a one-sentence goal instruction (for example, ``Group these
prompts by the type of manipulation they attempt'', or ``Identify the
topic of each text''). An orchestrator LLM proposes typed edits to a
working taxonomy through a dispatcher that validates every
precondition before mutating state and that records per-operation
failures without rolling back successful sibling edits. A stateless
judge LLM labels each item against the current taxonomy from item
text alone, with no access to the orchestrator's tool-call history
or prior label assignments. The judge runs on the cheap model and is
called once per item, so per-item inference cost is linear in corpus
size and the orchestrator cannot force a label past the judge.

Empirically (\S\ref{sec:evaluation}), with DeepSeek-v4-Flash for both
roles, \textsc{TaxonomyAgent} recovers the six-category expert taxonomy
of DarkBench~\citep{kran2025darkbench} at NMI $0.927 \pm 0.018$ with no
hint about the number of categories, leading every baseline asked to
group by the same manipulation type through its own interface by
$1.24\times$; on 20~Newsgroups it reaches NMI $0.740 \pm 0.055$, beating
a published topic-discovery pipeline by $1.22\times$. The result holds
under a second cheap model (gemini-3.1-flash-lite, NMI $0.884$).

Our contributions are fourfold. First, a tool for discovering
taxonomies of unstructured patterns in LLM outputs where the categories
are not known a priori (failure modes, reasoning strategies, error
types, jailbreak patterns). Second, a goal-instruction mechanism through
which the analyst steers discovery toward a chosen axis, differentiating
the system from topic-modeling pipelines that surface only the dominant
variation. Third, an evaluation on a real safety benchmark (DarkBench)
and a classical one (20~Newsgroups), where it matches or exceeds
published baselines at a fraction of the cost. Fourth, an open-source
artefact at \repourl{} with a \texttt{pip install} path, the paper's
default configuration, and a Streamlit Inspect tab that replays the
trace, partial taxonomies, and cost of any run.
