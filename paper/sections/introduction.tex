\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Constructing a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled corpus is a recurring
bottleneck in applied NLP. Annotation guidelines for supervised
classification, codebooks for qualitative research, summarisation schemas
for analyst-facing dashboards, and policy hierarchies for content
moderation all begin life as a manual taxonomy. Producing one typically
requires domain experts to read hundreds of documents, draft candidate
categories, argue over overlaps, and iterate until coverage and
disjointness are acceptable. The process is slow, expensive, and difficult
to reproduce, and the resulting taxonomy is often a brittle artefact that
nobody is incentivised to revisit when the underlying corpus drifts.

Recent work has shown that large language models (LLMs) can replace much
of this manual labour. TopicGPT~\citep{pham2024topicgpt} prompts an LLM
to generate topic names and definitions from sampled documents with a
refinement pass, while TnT-LLM~\citep{wan2024tntllm} stages a two-phase
pipeline that summarises a corpus into candidate labels and then distils
a lightweight classifier. Co-DETECT~\citep{codetect2025} is last year's
EMNLP System Demonstration on collaborative codebook construction with
LLMs and the closest peer to our submission; we sit at an orthogonal
operating point (taxonomy discovery from scratch over an unlabelled
corpus, without a seed sketch and without a human in the loop) and
discuss the relationship in \S\ref{sec:related}. Our contribution
is a structural commitment about how the proposer and the labeller
interact. The orchestrator proposes typed edits to a working taxonomy
through a validated dispatcher; a stateless judge re-derives each
item's category from the item text alone, never sees the
orchestrator's reasoning, and runs on the cheaper model. Two
properties follow. The per-item cost is linear in the corpus and
runs on the cheap model, so the publication and install configuration
is the same. The orchestrator cannot force a label past the judge,
because the judge re-derives it from item text.

Empirically, at the install default (DeepSeek-v4-Flash for both
orchestrator and judge), the system reaches NMI $0.740 \pm 0.055$
and ARI $0.489 \pm 0.085$ on 20~Newsgroups at $\$0.17$ per run,
beating a one-shot LLM baseline by $2.1\times$ on NMI and
$4.8\times$ on ARI and a model-matched embed-cluster-LLM
reimplementation of TnT-LLM by $1.5\times$ on NMI and $2.0\times$
on ARI (\S\ref{sec:eval-results-20ng}). A Sonnet 4.6 orchestrator
pair at eleven times the cost lands at NMI $0.656 \pm 0.012$,
inside the cheap pair's interval, so we make the cheap pair the
publication and install default. On the synthetic CoT-Pattern
corpus the system reaches NMI $0.944 \pm 0.081$ across three seeds
at $\$0.08$ per run, labelling on average $142$ of $149$ items at
$100\%$ accuracy (\S\ref{sec:eval-results-cot}).

\noindent Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A stateless re-deriving judge.} The labelling LLM
        sees only the current taxonomy and the item text and never
        the orchestrator's reasoning or prior labels, so the
        orchestrator cannot force a label past it. Cost is linear in
        the corpus on the cheap model.
  \item \textbf{A typed dispatcher with validate-before-mutate and
        per-operation failure isolation.} Each taxonomy edit is
        checked against every precondition before any state changes,
        and per-op failures within a batch do not roll back
        successful sibling ops. A malformed edit returns the
        unchanged taxonomy with a log entry the orchestrator can
        read on the next step.
  \item \textbf{An empirical demonstration that cheap-model both-roles
        dominates.} On 20NG and CoT-Pattern, DeepSeek-v4-Flash for
        both orchestrator and judge beats every comparator and
        matches a Claude Sonnet 4.6 orchestrator pair at eleven
        times the cost, contradicting the assumption that
        orchestrator quality is the bottleneck.
  \item \textbf{An open-source artefact at} \repourl{} with a
        \texttt{pip install} path, the install-default configuration
        used in the paper, and a Streamlit Inspect tab that replays
        the trace, partial taxonomies, and cost split of any run.
\end{itemize}
