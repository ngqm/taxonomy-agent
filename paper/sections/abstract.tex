We present \textsc{taxonomy\_agent}, an open-source system that
discovers and refines a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled text
corpus and ships with the operational tooling a stranger needs to
actually use it: live native provider cost accounting split by
orchestrator and judge, partial-progress streaming to disk so a killed
run leaves recoverable state, vendor-agnostic routing across any
chat-completions provider, and an interactive web UI for live
inspection. Beneath sits a two-tier agent: an orchestrator LLM
proposes edits through a typed revise dispatcher over six verbs
(\textit{add, rename, edit, merge, split, drop}), each validated
before mutation with per-operation failure isolation, while a cheap
parallel judge scores items and produces a don't-fit rate the
orchestrator uses as a termination self-report.
On a 487-document 20~Newsgroups subsample with three independently
sampled random seeds, \textsc{taxonomy\_agent} outperforms a one-shot
LLM baseline by $1.9\times$ on NMI and $3.3\times$ on ARI and retains
a $1.3\times$ NMI lead and $1.4\times$ ARI lead over an
embed-then-LLM-label baseline that is the strongest LLM-driven
comparator we run. On a 149-item synthetic corpus of failure
patterns in chain-of-thought reasoning, the system labels 109 of 149
items at $100\%$ accuracy and reaches $0.732$ coverage-adjusted purity
at parity with a $0.730$ ceiling from a 14-feature surface oracle and
$+23$ points above an unsupervised k-means baseline on the same
features.
We caution that the metrics alone cannot separate semantic discovery
from sharper surface extraction. Code, reproduction scripts, and the
corpora are at \repourl.
