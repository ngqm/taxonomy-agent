Turning a large collection of unlabelled text into a useful set of
categories is a recurring bottleneck, from safety researchers auditing
model interactions to analysts building annotation schemas. The
categories are not known in advance and depend on what the analyst
wants to group by, which existing tools do not let them choose:
topic-modeling pipelines surface the dominant topic and cannot
be steered, and evaluation benchmarks score models against a fixed
category set. We present \textsc{TaxonomyAgent}, an open-source
system that discovers a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled corpus
along a user-specified axis of variation. The analyst supplies the
corpus and one sentence naming the axis (for example, ``group these
prompts by the type of manipulation they attempt''); the system
returns a labelled taxonomy, a per-item classification, and a
replayable trace. On DarkBench, a benchmark of prompts that elicit six
manipulative dark patterns from AI assistants,
\textsc{TaxonomyAgent} recovers the six-category expert taxonomy at a
normalized mutual information (NMI) of 0.927 with no hint about the number of categories, ahead of
topic-modeling and LLM-clustering baselines even when each is asked to
group by the same manipulation type.
On 20~Newsgroups it reaches NMI 0.740 and beats a published
topic-discovery pipeline. Internally, two LLM roles are separated by a
structured contract: an orchestrator proposes typed taxonomy edits
while a stateless judge labels each item from item text alone, keeping
per-item inference cost linear in corpus size. Code, reproduction
scripts, and configuration are at \repourl.
