We present \textsc{taxonomy\_agent}, an open-source system that
discovers and refines a labelled taxonomy over an unlabelled text
corpus and ships with the operational tooling a stranger needs to
actually use it: live native provider cost accounting split by
orchestrator and judge, partial-progress streaming to disk so a killed
run leaves recoverable state, vendor-agnostic routing across any
chat-completions provider, and an interactive web UI for live
inspection. Beneath sits a two-tier agent: an orchestrator LLM
proposes edits through a typed revise dispatcher over six verbs
(\textit{add, rename, edit, merge, split, drop}), each validated
before mutation with per-operation failure isolation, while a cheap
parallel judge scores items and produces a don't-fit rate the
orchestrator uses as a termination self-report. We cross-check the
rate against external clustering metrics on 20~Newsgroups but stop
short of claiming it is a quantitatively validated leading indicator;
in practice it saturates at the floor.
On a 487-document 20~Newsgroups subsample with three independently
sampled random seeds, \textsc{taxonomy\_agent} outperforms a one-shot
LLM baseline by $1.9\times$ on NMI and $3.3\times$ on ARI, leads a
minimal iterative-proposal LLM baseline by larger margins, and
retains a $1.3\times$ NMI lead and $1.4\times$ ARI lead over an
embed-then-LLM-label baseline (16 sentence-transformer clusters, one
LLM naming call per cluster) that is the strongest LLM-driven
comparator we run. The lead over BERTopic at library defaults is wide
but narrows under a sweep of BERTopic's minimum cluster size that
closes most of the purity gap by splitting BERTopic into 60
micro-clusters, while the ARI lead holds at $2.6\times$. On a 149-item synthetic corpus of failure
patterns in chain-of-thought reasoning drawn from the 2024--2026
alignment literature, the system labels 109 of 149 items at 100\%\
accuracy and reaches 0.732 purity over the full corpus, sitting
$+27$ points on the labelled items and at parity at full coverage
against a 0.730 ceiling from a 14-feature surface classifier, and
$+23$ points above an unsupervised k-means baseline on the same
features; precision on labelled items stays at $100\%$ across all
four mathematical sub-domains.
We caution that the metrics alone cannot separate semantic discovery
from sharper surface extraction. Code, reproduction scripts, and the
corpora are at \repourl.
